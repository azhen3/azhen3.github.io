Cisco Product Management
Admin Device Catalog
Goal

Implemented a one stop shop for admins to manage Cisco’s product catalog, where they can add new products, edit existing product info, and publish products on the app for available ordering.

Client

Cisco Systems

Role

As the sole UX designer, my role was to lead the experience and visual implementation of this new platform.

Achievements

  • Autonomy: Gives multiple users, such as admins, the development team and catalog pricing team the ability to manage Cisco’s products from one source.
  • Transparent: All current and new products are listed in the catalog and editable by admins. This quick process helps promote products that are available for ordering.
  • Dynamic: Admins can rearrange products and categories however they want on the public page and also edit details for each.

The Problem

Cisco's collaboration learning platform allowed users to order products for their workplace environment. However, the information to manage product details derived from multiple data sources that contained duplicate information or were outdated. This became a problem, especially when the service team required changes in the product information.

The team took this opportunity to develop a new method for admins to manage all types of product details, which made it easy to edit without requesting the help from the development team.

Design Goals

The following goals derived from discussions with the engineers and the service team (intended users).

  • The new interface should include a wide range of actions, such as add, edit, publish and delete products, manage the public details page, and determine pricing.
  • There should only be a singular database that includes all the updated details of each product.
  • The product catalog should be fully visible, meaning that every item in the database is viewable on the admin interface.

Ideation

Initial Explorations

The first challenge was to figure out how to display both categories and the devices that fall into them. The team first considered a WYSIWYG editor for both, but it became too cluttered when determining how product pricing might be displayed.

Because the team wanted to give admins the ability to rearrange products within a category as well as categories themselves, this design became clunky and slow. Likewise, the fact that all products were pulled from the database to display here caused the accordion function to break. Thus, I began iterating on other options.

Solution

I decided that, in order to simplify the actions, I had to break up the categories view and the products view. In this way, the workflow [show workflow image] would be from a high level categories view, to the products listed within a category, then to the specific product details page. This allowed category-specific or product-specific actions to be displayed on its respective page, and still made it easy to navigate between the pages.
