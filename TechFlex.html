Problem

Physical therapists currently use bubble or digital inclinometers to measure lumbar range of motion.
Two inclinometers are positioned on T12 and S1 spinous processes. Baseline degree and final degree are recorded at neutral position and maximum flexion, respectively.
Difference between the two degrees is the lumbar range of motion (ROM). ROM can also be measured for cervical or thoracic vertebrae.
Physical therapists must quickly do mental math with the recorded measurements while stabilizing both devices and correcting the patient's form. This greatly diminishes their quality of care and may also compromise their ability to diagnose accurate measurements.

User Research

We observed the routine of measuring lumbar range of motion (ROM) on multiple patients and shared our findings with Dr. Walsh, physical therapist at University of North Georgia (UNG). We discussed the user needs, both of the clinician and the patient, and listed the most valued specifications below.

Usability

  • Under 5 min setup time
  • Short set of instructions
  • Low number of parts

Accuracy

  • Minimize hip movement error
  • Distinct landmarks on vertebrae

Accessibility

  • Under 5 lbs
  • Used in any clinical setting

Durability

  • Lasts over 5 years
  • Sanitizable

Price

  • Under $50

Ideation

First Prototype

I wrote the code for the Flora to calculate absolute position of each accelerometer and the difference in orientation in three axes. This real-time display was used to test and debug the components. We tested the accelerometers using cardboard to mimic the spine, with the distances between each matching anthropometric spinous process height.

Our first iteration was made of fabric lining which attached all the components together, producing a single device. We took this prototype to Dr. Walsh for first generation testing.

Second Prototype

After Dr. Walsh’s feedback, we decided to simplify to only the necessary components for accurate measurement. We internalized all the components into a 3D-printed casing, leaving one remote accelerometer to attach to T12 location. The calibration code zeros out the angles when the button is pressed, allowing for multiple measurements from a certain position.

Final Design

Accuracy

Measurements were recorded on eight subjects, with three trials each. The results yielded 0.42% standard error in precision and 20% higher accuracy than current methods (double inclinometer).

Accessibility

The total weight of the device is less than one pound, which exceeds our client’s needs. The gold standard for ROM measurement is using at X-ray, which is expensive and impractical for a small clinical setting.

Durability

We designed and 3D-printed the outer casing from ABS plastic. The two parts fit nicely into each other, eliminating the use for screws. The inner electronic components can be disassemblied and replaced when needed, allowing longer usage without having to discard the entire device.

Price

As a product, the device would contain custom circuitboard and injection-molded plastic for the casing. This market price would be significantly lower than the prototype cost.

Usability

For the physical therapist, he would significantly decrease setup time by simply attaching the base station around the patient's hip (S1 location). The remote accelerometer can attach to electrode pads through button snaps, and can be snapped to the T12 vertebrae. The PT will be hands-free so he can focus on instructing the patient, observe posture, and stabilize the patient’s hips if needed.

Future Improvements

TechFlex is currently in testing phase as Dr. Walsh is using the device on his patients and students. We plan to build upon his user feedback and continue to improve the device through the following functions below.

Bluetooth wireless connection for remote accelerometers

Custom circuitboard

Data storage and predictive analytics
