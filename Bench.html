Problem

The transfer bench is a medical device that allows individuals to safely transfer into and out of the bathtub, providing a comfortable area to sit while showering. With a market of over 6.8 million Americans annually utilizing assistive medical devices, a redesign of this medical device is necessary, given the issues with current transfer benches.

Most transfer benches are only able to address the issues associated with the elderly and orthopedic patients. They often fail to consider the needs of other users, such as paraplegics. Paraplegia is the condition characterized by immobility and lack of sensation in the lower body, which makes activities of daily living (ADLs) difficult and even dangerous.

Our team developed a solution to provide a safe, comfortable showering experience for young, active individuals with paraplegia and lack of reliance on caregivers.

Market Research

We bought the cheapest portable transfer bench ($65) and tested it out ourselves to understand the underlying issues with the device. The device was lightweight, customizable to height, easily cleaned, and able to be disassembled. However, the bigger issues included instability due to uneven height of inner tub and floor, slippery seat, and difficult to assemble.

User Research

We spoke with Dr. Cassie Mitchell, a Georgia Tech professor and Paralympic discus thrower. She spoke about her problems with transferring into and out of the shower, mentioning that slipping and falling is her biggest fear. The devices in market do not address the safety concerns of paraplegics- the cheap quality feels unsafe and often will cause instability from the limited adjustable seat height.

We developed a pairwise comparison chart of design specifications based on interviews with paraplegics and the elderly. The criteria are crosslisted with each other, and the highest total represents factors of highest priority.

To quantify these objectives, metrics were established in comparison with currently available devices. To measure the usefulness, the time it would take for a paraplegic to transfer into the bathtub safely was considered. The metric for ease of set up was based on the time and the amount of parts required. For durability and safety, we calculated maximum deformations and loads, which determined how the design should be structured and what materials should be used.

Ideation

The first design concepts ranged from minimal alteration to the handlebars of the bathtub to a complete redesign of the user environment. These concepts provided a wide scope of what was or not feasible, which allowed us to converge our thinking to more simple designs.

With the insight gained from interviews with Dr. Mitchell and other paraplegics, we developed three main concept models:

  • Retroversion (ergonomic curvature of the seat)
  • Portable version (fan)
  • Complete bathtub redesign

After discussing with various users, we discovered that the retroversion on a stationary device (for home use) would provide a safe and comfortable method of transfer, and was reasonable in terms of cost and efficiency.

Prototype

We modeled the seat panels from wood ribs and stringers to strengthen the prototype for testing. The wood was lasercut, while the aluminum frames were modified from the original device. Two clamps were attached to both legs on one end to mimic the clamping mechanism.

Solution

We developed our solution which improved current products in several ways:

  • Expanding the backrest to the length of the bench to prevent falling or imbalance
  • Seat curvature based on anthropometric data to prevent slipping
  • Handles in seat for better stabilization when showering
  • Quick release height adjustment
  • Quick release clamps to bathtub edge

These changes allowed universality and stability of the device, making the Glide Slide adjustable to any type of bathtub environment without compromising form and durability.
